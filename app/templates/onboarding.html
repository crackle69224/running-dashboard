Welcome to Zoned Running

Zoned Running is built around one core idea: your heart rate is a better real-time signal of training stress than pace or distance alone. Two runs at the same pace can mean completely different things for your body depending on heart rate — and tracking by zone helps you train the right system on the right day.

There are three main approaches researchers use to describe how training time should be split across heart rate zones. Pick the one you want to follow — you can change this later as your training progresses.

Polarized
Mostly easy, occasionally very hard, nothing in between

~75-80% of training stays low intensity (Zone 1-2), ~15-20% is genuinely hard (Zone 4-5), with almost no time at "moderately hard." Each run has one clear job — build the aerobic base, or push the top end.

Polarized training allocates roughly 75% of training time below the lactate threshold, 15-20% well above it, and only about 5% to the moderate zone in between (Seiler & Kjerland, 2006).

A 2024 systematic review of 14 studies found this combination of high-volume low intensity with a moderate dose of high intensity was most beneficial for improving VO2max, peak oxygen uptake, and running economy in endurance athletes.

A controlled comparison of four training concepts found polarized training produced the greatest increases in VO2peak, time-to-exhaustion, and peak power compared to threshold, high-intensity-only, or high-volume training alone.

Mechanistically: low-intensity work drives aerobic oxidation adaptations, while high-intensity work stresses the glycolytic and ATP-CP energy systems — genuinely different physiological pathways. The near-empty middle zone is intentional, since training there doesn't strongly stimulate either pathway.

Pyramidal
Mostly easy, a meaningful dose of moderate, a little hard

~75-85% Zone 1, ~10-25% Zone 2 (tempo), ~5-10% Zone 3-4. The most commonly observed pattern among real elite athletes — slightly less extreme than polarized, with more room for "comfortably hard" running.

The pyramidal model keeps low intensity dominant but allows more time in the moderate zone than polarized training — roughly 50-75% Zone 1, 25-30% Zone 2, 5-10% Zone 3.

A large retrospective review of 175 documented elite training distributions found 89 were pyramidal, compared to 65 polarized and only 8 threshold — making pyramidal the single most common real-world pattern among elite endurance athletes.

A direct 16-week controlled study in well-trained runners found something worth knowing if you plan to train for many months: runners who trained pyramidal first and then switched to polarized in the second half showed the largest improvements across VO2peak, lactate thresholds, and 5K time. The sequence of models across a training block may matter as much as which single model you pick.

Threshold
Heavy emphasis on "comfortably hard" steady efforts

Most training happens right around your lactate threshold, without much truly easy or truly hard work. Common among recreational runners by accident, and the least supported of the three models in head-to-head research.

Really?

Across 175 documented elite training distributions, only 8 were threshold-dominant — making it the rarest pattern among athletes performing at a high level. A 2018 systematic review concluded that pyramidal and polarized training are both more effective than threshold training, although a few elite marathoners do still use it.

The likely reason: this "comfortably hard" zone is demanding enough to require real recovery, but not hard enough to drive the same top-end adaptations as genuine high-intensity work — sometimes called training's "no man's land."

One honest caveat: if your weekly training time is limited, splitting time between easy/moderate and moderate/hard with a small dose of fast running may work about as well as a strict polarized split. This isn't a wrong choice — it's just the least-supported default for most runners with room to train more.

Does my body type or personality affect which model is better for me?

Short answer: there's emerging evidence that people genuinely respond differently to these models, but the strongest known predictor right now isn't a personality or body trait — it's training experience.

A 2025 machine-learning study split 120 recreational marathon runners into 16-week pyramidal or polarized groups and found four distinct response patterns: about a third responded better to polarized, about a third responded better to pyramidal, ~19% responded well to either, and ~18% didn't respond strongly to either as structured. Training experience was the strongest predictor of which approach worked best for a given runner.

There's also a real physiological difference between the two: pyramidal training tends to produce larger gains in cardiac stroke volume (better efficiency at submaximal effort), while polarized training tends to produce larger VO2max and neuromuscular gains that show up more in the late stages of a race.

A practical, low-risk guideline several coaches converge on: newer runners tend to do better starting with a pyramidal approach, since it has more built-in margin for error, and trying a polarized or threshold approach after 6-12 months of consistent pyramidal training.

Bottom line: there's no clean "if you have trait X, pick model Y" rule yet backed by strong evidence. Your training history matters more than we can currently predict from any single physical or personality trait. You can change your model at any time as you gather more data on how you personally respond.
